2. Cost Accounting Framework

Lifecycle Boundary

The model covers the full lifecycle from substantial completion, including all pre-construction costs (land, design, permitting), construction, financing, operations, and end-of-horizon disposition. For residential archetypes (SF, MF) this is 30 years of ownership; for the School archetype this is 50 years (matching NIST Handbook 135 / ASTM E917 LCCA standards for institutional buildings). This represents a complete "cradle to transfer" owner/district cost perspective.

Treatment of Land

Land is included at acquisition cost as a one-time expenditure at year 0. No appreciation or depreciation is modeled, as this is a cost model, not an investment model. For SF, lot cost is derived from NAHB's reported 18.5% share of sale price. For MF, land is estimated at 10% of total development cost per unit.

Treatment of Financing

Mortgage interest is the cost of borrowing and represents the single largest cost category for most owners. We model:

  • SF: 80% LTV conventional 30-year fixed at 6.75%. Total interest over 30 years: $448,770.
  • MF: 70% LTV agency permanent loan at 6.5%, 30-year amortization. Total interest: $267,600/unit.
  • School: 100% GO municipal bond at 5.0% coupon, 25-year level debt service. Total interest: ~$11,765,000. Issuance costs: $471,000 (2% of par).
  • Construction loan interest (SF/MF): modeled as a one-time pre-construction cost.
  • Origination fees/points: one-time cost at closing.
  • Principal repayment is excluded: it builds equity (residential) or retires debt (School), not a pure cost.

3. Discounting & Time Treatment

Time Horizon Justification

Residential (SF, MF): 30 years matches the standard mortgage term, aligns with DOE/BTO analysis periods, and captures 1-2 full replacement cycles for most major building systems (roof, HVAC).

School: 50 years matches NIST Handbook 135 and ASTM E917 LCCA standards for institutional buildings. School buildings are designed for longer service lives, and 50 years captures 2 full HVAC replacement cycles, 2 roof replacements, and 3-4 flooring/technology refresh cycles.

Real vs. Nominal

All analysis is in constant 2024 dollars (real terms). No nominal escalation is modeled. The discount rate is a real rate (net of general inflation).

Default Discount Rate

Default: 3% real, consistent with OMB Circular A-94 guidance for cost-effectiveness analysis and DOE's standard practice. User-adjustable from 1% to 7%.

Present Value Formulas

Recurring annual cost:
PV = C × [(1 - (1+r)-n) / r]
At 3% for 30 years: PV = C × 19.6004; for 50 years: PV = C × 25.7298

Periodic replacement at year t (mid-year convention):
PV = Creplacement / (1+r)t

End-of-horizon cost at year 30:
PV = C30 / (1+r)30 = C30 / 2.4273 at 3%

Replacement Cycle Handling

Major systems are replaced according to published life expectancy data (NAHB, ASHRAE). Replacement costs are expressed in 2024 USD and discounted from the year of replacement. Mid-year convention: replacements assumed at mid-year of scheduled year.

4. Data Normalization

Conversion Rules

  • Per-building to per-ft²: divide by archetype area (2,200 for SF; 950 for MF per unit; 75,000 for School)
  • Per-unit to per-building (MF): multiply by 100 units
  • School: All values expressed per building (total); $/ft² provided as secondary metric in provenance
  • Year conversion: value2024 = valuesource_year × (CPI2024 / CPIsource_year)

Triangulation Method

When multiple sources provide values for the same category:

  1. Federal/Census data preferred for quantities (sizes, counts, rates).
  2. NAHB/industry surveys preferred for cost breakdowns and shares.
  3. Academic/consultant sources used for friction, rework, and schedule data.
  4. When sources disagree by <20%: weighted average (weight by recency and sample size).
  5. When sources disagree by >20%: range reported (low/base/high), midpoint used as base, flagged in provenance.

5. Friction Definition

Formal Definition

Lifecycle friction = the excess cost, time, and coordination burden beyond the theoretical minimum required to achieve a given lifecycle outcome. It measures where value is lost to waiting, rework, miscommunication, and uncertainty.

Components

ComponentMeaningMeasurement
Queue timeWaiting/approval delays not adding valueDays waiting (permits, inspections, materials, scheduling)
Rework rateRedesign, resubmittals, corrections% of stage cost or effort attributed to rework
Coordination (handoffs)Cross-actor communication burdenNumber of handoffs; coordination complexity score (1-5)
UncertaintyOutcome unpredictability, inspection riskQualitative 1-5 scale based on literature

Friction Score Formula

Friction_Score = 0.30 × norm(queue_time) + 0.30 × norm(rework_rate) + 0.20 × norm(coordination) + 0.20 × norm(uncertainty)

Where:
• norm(x) = (x - xmin) / (xmax - xmin) across all cells, scaled to [0, 1]
• Weights: queue=0.30, rework=0.30, coordination=0.20, uncertainty=0.20
• Score range: 0 (minimal friction) to 1.0 (maximum friction)

Weight rationale: Queue time and rework are weighted higher (0.30 each) because they are the two most directly measurable and impactful components. Coordination and uncertainty are weighted at 0.20 each as they rely more on qualitative assessment.

Interpretability: The formula is fully transparent. A score of 0.82 for MF plan review means that process has very high friction across all four dimensions. The primary drivers are queue time (60 days) and rework rate (55% first-submission rejection).

"Estimated" Label

A value is labeled "estimated" when no direct source exists for the specific archetype/category, and the value is derived by analogy, ratio, or reasoning. All estimated values show a range and supporting rationale.

Top 10 Assumptions Driving Results

  1. Mortgage interest rate (6.5-6.75%): Single largest cost driver. A 1% change in rate shifts total cost by ~$50k-$80k for SF.
  2. Property tax rate (1.0-1.1% SF, 0.67% MF): High geographic variation; 2nd largest annual cost.
  3. Maintenance rate (1% of value): Conservative; actual deferred maintenance is common.
  4. Construction hard cost per ft² ($114/ft² SF, $139/ft² MF): ~30% regional variation.
  5. Land cost ($80k SF, $30k/unit MF): Can vary 5x or more by location.
  6. Discount rate (3% default): Changing to 7% reduces discounted total by ~35%.
  7. Replacement cycle timing: Roof at 25yr, HVAC at 15-17yr assumed.
  8. Builder/developer profit margin (7.5-10%): Market-cycle dependent.
  9. Insurance cost ($2,400/yr SF, $900/unit MF): Rising rapidly; could double in 30 years.
  10. HOA/management costs: MF management is significant; SF HOA is minimal but assumed present.

7. Limitations

  • Geographic averaging: "Typical U.S." masks enormous regional variation. Costs in coastal metros can be 2-5x higher than rural areas.
  • Market cycle sensitivity: Construction costs, interest rates, and land values fluctuate significantly. Values reflect circa 2023-2024 conditions.
  • Regulatory variation: Permitting costs and timelines vary dramatically by jurisdiction. National averages obscure this reality.
  • Homeowner behavior: Maintenance spending varies enormously. We model "adequate" maintenance; actual behavior often involves significant deferral.
  • Constant quality: The model assumes no upgrades, energy retrofits, or additions over 30 years.
  • Single financing scenario: One mortgage type/rate. Actual financing varies enormously (FHA, VA, ARM, etc.).
  • No tax benefits: Mortgage interest deduction, property tax deductions, and depreciation (MF) are excluded.
  • Not for project budgeting: This model is for policy analysis and lifecycle understanding, NOT for predicting specific project costs.
