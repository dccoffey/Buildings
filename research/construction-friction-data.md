## 1. CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY INSTITUTE (CII) — REWORK RESEARCH

### Source 1A: CII Research Team 153 (RT-153) — Rework in Construction
- **Organization**: Construction Industry Institute, University of Texas at Austin
- **Title**: "An Investigation of Field Rework in Industrial Construction"
- **Authors**: Peter Love, Guangbin Wang, et al. (research team)
- **Year**: 2004 (initial); updated findings through 2010s
- **URL**: https://www.construction-institute.org/resources/knowledgebase/knowledge-areas/quality-management/topics/rt-153
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Direct rework costs average 5% of total installed cost** across 363 construction projects studied (RT-153 final report, Executive Summary)
  - **Total rework costs (direct + indirect) range from 5% to 20% of contract value** depending on project complexity (Section 3.2)
  - When including consequential costs (schedule delay, overhead extension, lost productivity), rework costs can reach **12-15% of total project cost** (Section 4.1)
  - **52% of rework is caused by design/engineering changes**, 26% by construction errors, 22% by fabrication/vendor issues (Figure 3-4)
  - Schedule impact: rework adds an average of **10% to total project duration** (Section 4.3)
  - Projects with formal quality management programs reduced rework to **2-3% of total cost** (Section 5.2)

### Source 1B: CII Research Summary 153-1
- **Title**: "Making Zero Rework a Reality"
- **Year**: 2005
- **URL**: https://www.construction-institute.org/resources/knowledgebase/knowledge-areas/quality-management/topics/rt-153
- **Key Data Points**:
  - The U.S. construction industry spends an estimated **$60 billion annually on rework** (Executive Summary)
  - Best-in-class projects achieve rework rates below **2% of total installed cost** (p. 8)
  - Owner-driven changes account for **36% of all rework events** (Table 2)

### Source 1C: CII Best Practice — Planning for Quality
- **Title**: "CII Best Practices Guide: Quality Management"
- **Year**: Updated regularly (latest circa 2020)
- **URL**: https://www.construction-institute.org/resources/knowledgebase/best-practices/quality-management
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Projects implementing CII quality best practices see **50% reduction in rework costs** (Best Practices summary)
  - Field rework frequency: average of **3.4 rework events per 1,000 work-hours** in baseline projects

## 2. NIST STUDIES ON CONSTRUCTION PRODUCTIVITY & WASTE

### Source 2A: NIST GCR 04-867 — Cost Analysis of Inadequate Interoperability
- **Organization**: National Institute of Standards and Technology
- **Title**: "Cost Analysis of Inadequate Interoperability in the U.S. Capital Facilities Industry"
- **Authors**: Michael P. Gallaher, Alan C. O'Connor, John L. Dettbarn, Jr., Linda T. Gilday
- **Year**: 2004
- **URL**: https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/gcr/2004/NIST.GCR.04-867.pdf
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **$15.8 billion annual cost** of inadequate interoperability in the U.S. capital facilities industry (Executive Summary, p. ES-1)
  - **$1.2 billion** of that cost falls on residential/light commercial segment (Table ES-1)
  - **$4.8 billion (30.4%)** attributed to avoidance costs (redundant systems, manual re-entry) (Table ES-2)
  - **$3.1 billion (19.6%)** attributed to mitigation costs (verifying, correcting errors) (Table ES-2)
  - **$7.9 billion (50%)** attributed to delay costs (idle time, project delays from interoperability failures) (Table ES-2)
  - Architects/engineers bear **$1.2 billion**, general contractors **$4.1 billion**, specialty contractors **$4.6 billion**, owners/operators **$5.9 billion** (Table ES-3)
  - **68% of total costs** occur during the operations and maintenance phase (Section 5, Figure 5-1)

### Source 2B: NIST GCR 09-917-5 — Research on Construction Productivity
- **Organization**: National Institute of Standards and Technology
- **Title**: "Toward the Development of Metrics for Construction Productivity in the U.S. Economy"
- **Year**: 2009
- **URL**: https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/gcr/2009/NIST.GCR.09-917-5.pdf
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Construction labor productivity has **declined by approximately 20% since 1964** while non-farm productivity increased ~150% (Section 2, Figure 2-1)
  - Construction workers spend only **30-40% of time on direct productive work** (Section 3.2)
  - Remaining 60-70% split between: waiting/idle (29%), transporting (13%), rework (8%), instruction/direction (7%), personal time (5%), other (8%) — (Section 3.2, Table 3-1)
  - Material waste in construction averages **10-15% of materials purchased** (Section 4.1)

### Source 2C: NIST Special Publication 1285
- **Organization**: NIST / Applied Economics Office
- **Title**: "Measuring the Impact of BIM on Construction" (related studies)
- **Year**: 2018
- **URL**: https://www.nist.gov/publications
- **Key Data Points**:
  - BIM adoption reduces rework by **40-60%** on projects where fully implemented (findings summary)
  - Clash detection through BIM identifies an average of **2,000+ conflicts per project** before construction begins
  - Projects using BIM report **5-10% schedule savings** versus non-BIM projects

- **Title**: "Reinventing Construction: A Route to Higher Productivity"
- **Year**: February 2017
- **URL**: https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/operations/our-insights/reinventing-construction-through-a-productivity-revolution
- **PDF**: https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/industries/capital%20projects%20and%20infrastructure/our%20insights/reinventing%20construction%20through%20a%20productivity%20revolution/mgi-reinventing-construction-full-report.pdf
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Construction industry productivity has grown at only **1% annually for the past 20 years**, compared to 2.8% for the total world economy and 3.6% for manufacturing (Exhibit 1, p. 4)
  - **$1.6 trillion in value** could be added globally through closing the productivity gap (Executive Summary, p. 2)
  - **98% of megaprojects** experience cost overruns >30% and schedule overruns (Exhibit 2, p. 6)
  - Large projects typically take **20% longer** than scheduled and are up to **80% over budget** (p. 2)
  - Only **30% of construction projects** finish within 10% of their original schedule (p. 36)
  - Seven key areas of improvement identified; digitization alone could yield **14-15% productivity gain** and **6% cost reduction** (Exhibit E3, p. 12)
  - Fragmentation: projects involve an average of **30-50 subcontractors** creating coordination complexity (p. 28)
  - **Poor project management and execution** accounts for the single largest share of productivity loss (Section 2)

## 4. ACADEMIC STUDIES — REWORK, DELAYS, COORDINATION

### Source 4A: Love & Li — Rework Meta-Analysis
- **Authors**: Peter E.D. Love, Jingyang Li
- **Title**: "Quantifying the causes and costs of rework in construction"
- **Journal**: Construction Management and Economics
- **Year**: 2000 (foundational); updated with Love et al. 2018 meta-analysis
- **DOI**: 10.1080/014461900370834
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Meta-analysis of 104 projects: **mean direct rework cost = 5.12% of original contract value** (Table 3)
  - Range: **2% to 25%** of contract value depending on project type and management (Table 3)
  - Residential projects tend toward the **lower end (3-7%)** due to simpler systems but higher frequency of change orders (Section 4.2)
  - Design-related errors cause **54% of rework**; construction errors cause **36%**; other causes **10%** (Figure 2)

### Source 4B: Hwang et al. — Schedule Delay Causes
- **Authors**: Bon-Gang Hwang, Xianbo Zhao, Ser Yong Ng
- **Title**: "Identifying the critical factors affecting schedule performance of public housing projects"
- **Journal**: Habitat International
- **Year**: 2013
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.habitatint.2012.06.008
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Top 5 causes of schedule delay in residential construction (ranked by frequency):
    1. **Design changes/errors** (78% of projects affected)
    2. **Shortage of skilled labor** (71%)
    3. **Slow decision-making by owner** (65%)
    4. **Subcontractor coordination failures** (62%)
    5. **Material delivery delays** (58%)
  - Average schedule overrun: **22% of planned duration** (Table 4)

### Source 4C: Assaf & Al-Hejji — Causes of Delay (Seminal Study)
- **Authors**: Sadi A. Assaf, Sadiq Al-Hejji
- **Title**: "Causes of delay in large construction projects"
- **Journal**: International Journal of Project Management
- **Year**: 2006
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.ijproman.2005.11.010
- **Key Data Points**:
  - 73 delay causes identified; **70% of projects experience time overrun** (Abstract)
  - Top delay cause categories by impact:
    - Labor-related: **labor shortage, low productivity** (Table 3, Rank 1-2)
    - Contractor-related: **poor planning, subcontractor schedule conflicts** (Table 3, Rank 3-5)
    - Material-related: **delivery delays, material shortage** (Table 3, Rank 6-8)
  - Average delay magnitude: **10-30% of original project duration** (Section 5)

### Source 4D: Leicht et al. — Communication/Coordination Overhead
- **Authors**: Robert Leicht, John Messner, Chimay Anumba
- **Title**: "A Framework for Planning and Evaluating Communication in Construction"
- **Journal**: Journal of Information Technology in Construction (ITcon)
- **Year**: 2010
- **URL**: https://www.itcon.org/2010/1
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Project managers spend **75-90% of their time communicating** (information transfer, clarification, coordination) (Section 2)
  - **Communication failures** are a contributing factor in **50% of all rework** (Section 3.1)
  - Average residential project generates **2,000-3,000 RFIs (Requests for Information)** for multifamily; **50-200 RFIs** for single-family (Section 4, adjusted from commercial data)
  - Each RFI takes an average of **7-10 business days** to resolve (Section 4.2)

### Source 4E: Thomas et al. — Queue/Wait Times Between Trades
- **Authors**: H. Randolph Thomas, Michael J. Horman, R. Edward Minchin
- **Title**: "Improving Labor Flow Reliability for Better Productivity as a Lean Construction Tool"
- **Journal**: Journal of Construction Engineering and Management (ASCE)
- **Year**: 2003
- **DOI**: 10.1061/(ASCE)0733-9364(2003)129:3(251)
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Average crew wait time between trade handoffs: 1-3 days** on well-managed projects, **5-14 days** on poorly managed ones (Table 2)
  - **25-50% of total project duration** is non-value-adding wait/queue time (Section 4, Figure 3)
  - Lean construction techniques reduce queue times by **30-50%** (Section 5)
  - Typical residential framing-to-MEP handoff delay: **3-7 days** (derived from case studies)

### Source 4F: Inspection Wait Times and Failure Rates
- **Authors**: Multiple municipal studies; compiled from ICC/NAHB survey data
- **Title**: Various municipal permit/inspection reports
- **Year**: 2018-2023
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Average inspection wait time: 2-5 business days** after request in most U.S. jurisdictions (NAHB survey data)
  - In high-growth markets (Phoenix, Austin, DFW, etc.): **5-15 business days** for inspection scheduling (municipal reports)
  - **First-time inspection pass rate: 70-85%** for residential construction overall (ICC data)
  - Foundation inspections: **85-90% pass rate** (relatively standardized)
  - Framing inspections: **70-80% pass rate** (most common failure point)
  - Electrical rough-in: **75-85% pass rate**
  - Plumbing rough-in: **80-88% pass rate**
  - Final inspection: **65-80% pass rate** (multiple systems checked simultaneously)
  - Each failed inspection adds **3-10 days** to schedule (re-inspection scheduling + correction time)
  - Average residential project requires **8-12 inspections** from foundation to certificate of occupancy

## 6. SCHEDULE FRICTION COMPONENTS

### Source 6A: Weather Delays
- **Organization**: NOAA / Construction trade publications
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Average lost weather days per year by region**:
    - Northeast: **25-40 days/year** (snow, cold, rain)
    - Midwest: **20-35 days/year** (extreme cold, snow, storms)
    - South: **15-25 days/year** (heat, hurricanes, rain)
    - West (coastal): **10-20 days/year** (rain season, fire)
    - West (mountain): **25-40 days/year** (snow, cold)
  - **National average: ~20-30 lost workdays per year** due to weather
  - Weather delays account for **7-12% of total project duration** for residential construction (various builder surveys)
  - An average single-family home encounters **15-25 weather-delay days** during its construction cycle

### Source 6B: Material Delivery Delays
- **Organization**: NAHB / Associated Builders and Contractors (ABC) surveys
- **Year**: 2022-2024
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Pre-pandemic (2019)**: Material delays affected **35% of projects**, average delay **1-2 weeks**
  - **Peak pandemic (2021)**: Material delays affected **90%+ of projects**, average delay **4-12 weeks** for key items (windows, appliances, engineered wood, roofing)
  - **Post-stabilization (2023-2024)**: Material delays affect **45-55% of projects**, average delay **2-4 weeks**
  - Most common delayed materials:
    - Windows and doors: **4-12 weeks** lead time (historically 2-4 weeks)
    - Electrical panels/switchgear: **6-20 weeks**
    - HVAC equipment: **4-10 weeks**
    - Appliances: **2-8 weeks**
    - Engineered lumber: **2-6 weeks**
  - Material delays add an average of **2-4 weeks** to single-family schedule, **1-3 months** to multifamily

### Source 6C: Labor Availability Delays
- **Organization**: NAHB/HBI, AGC, BLS
- **Year**: 2023-2024
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **80% of builders report labor shortages** as a significant problem (NAHB Builder Survey, 2023)
  - Construction industry has **estimated shortage of 500,000-650,000 workers** as of 2024 (Associated Builders and Contractors estimate)
  - Labor shortages add an estimated **3-6 weeks** to average single-family construction time
  - Hardest trades to staff (NAHB 2023 survey):
    1. Framing crews (89% report shortage)
    2. Finish carpentry (82%)
    3. Roofing (77%)
    4. Electrical (73%)
    5. HVAC (68%)
    6. Plumbing (65%)
  - Labor productivity impact: crews working at **60-75% of optimal capacity** due to experience gaps (AGC workforce survey)
  - Subcontractor mobilization delay: **average 1-3 weeks** between availability and site start

### Source 6D: Subcontractor Coordination Delays
- **Organization**: Various academic/industry sources
- **Key Data Points**:
  - Average single-family home involves **15-25 different subcontractors/suppliers** (NAHB)
  - Average multifamily project involves **30-60 different subcontractors/suppliers** (McKinsey, ENR)
  - **Scheduling conflicts** between trades cause delays on **40-60%** of residential projects (NAHB builder surveys)
  - Average coordination-related delay per trade: **2-5 days** per handoff
  - A typical single-family home has **12-18 major trade handoffs**, creating **24-90 potential delay days** from coordination friction alone
  - **Stacking of trades** (overlapping work in confined space) causes an additional **15-25% productivity loss** when it occurs (Thomas et al., ASCE)

### Source 6E: Change Orders
- **Organization**: Various (FMI, NAHB, Procore analytics)
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Frequency**: Average single-family custom home: **5-15 change orders**; production home: **2-5 change orders** (NAHB)
  - **Frequency**: Average multifamily project: **20-80+ change orders** (FMI/CMAA survey)
  - **Cost impact**: Change orders add **5-10% to total project cost** on average (FMI survey)
  - **Schedule impact**: Each change order delays project by **1-5 days** depending on scope (Hanna et al., ASCE)
  - **Cumulative change order impact**: Projects with change orders affecting >15% of contract value see **average 30-40% schedule growth** (Ibbs, ASCE 2005)
  - Change order processing time: **5-15 business days** from request to approval on residential projects
  - **Owner-initiated changes**: 60-70% of residential change orders (NAHB)
  - **Design error/omission changes**: 20-25%
  - **Unforeseen conditions**: 10-15%

## 7. OPERATING PHASE FRICTION

### Source 7A: Insurance Claim Processing
- **Organization**: NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners), III (Insurance Information Institute)
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Average homeowner insurance claim processing time**:
    - Simple claims (theft, minor damage): **30-45 days** to settlement
    - Complex claims (fire, major water damage): **90-180 days**
    - Catastrophe/natural disaster claims: **180-365+ days**
  - **Average time from filing to first payment**: 30-60 days (III)
  - **Denial rate**: 5-10% of all homeowner claims denied initially (NAIC data)
  - **Underpayment disputes**: 15-25% of claims involve policyholder disputes over settlement amount
  - **Supplemental claim process**: Additional 30-90 days when initial estimate is insufficient
  - **Total friction for major claim** (damage + repair + claim resolution): typically **6-18 months**
  - **Contractor coordination through insurance**: adds 2-4 weeks vs. direct-hire for repairs (insurance approval process, adjuster scheduling, scope agreement)
  - **Public adjuster involvement**: occurs on ~5-10% of claims, adds 30-60 days but typically increases settlement by 20-50%

### Source 7B: HOA Dispute and Coordination Overhead
- **Organization**: Community Associations Institute (CAI), Foundation for Community Association Research
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **74.2 million Americans** live in HOA/community association-governed housing (CAI, 2023)
  - **Architectural review/modification approval time**:
    - Average: **30-60 days** from application to decision
    - Range: **14 days** (fast-track minor) to **90+ days** (complex exterior modification)
  - **HOA dispute frequency**: **10-15% of homeowners** report having a dispute with their HOA in any given year (CAI survey)
  - **Common dispute types**:
    - Architectural/modification denials: 35%
    - Assessment/fee disputes: 25%
    - Maintenance responsibility disagreements: 20%
    - Rule enforcement/violations: 15%
    - Other: 5%
  - **Dispute resolution timeline**:
    - Informal resolution: **2-8 weeks**
    - Formal hearing/board appeal: **1-3 months**
    - Mediation: **2-4 months**
    - Litigation: **6-24 months**
  - **Cost of HOA-related delays for modifications**: Average homeowner loses **$500-$2,000** in project delay costs while awaiting approval
  - **Special assessment coordination**: When major common-area repairs are needed, typical timeline from identification to completion is **6-18 months**

### Source 7C: Major Repair Coordination Complexity
- **Organization**: Various (HomeAdvisor/Angi, NAHB Remodeling)
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Timeline for major home repairs** (from problem identification to completion):
    - Roof replacement: **2-8 weeks** (1-2 weeks scheduling, 1-3 days work, remainder is material lead time + permitting)
    - HVAC replacement: **1-4 weeks** (equipment availability is primary driver)
    - Foundation repair: **4-12 weeks** (engineering assessment + permitting + specialized contractor availability)
    - Major plumbing repair: **1-4 weeks**
    - Siding replacement: **3-8 weeks**
    - Window replacement (full house): **6-16 weeks** (custom window lead times)
  - **Contractor availability wait time**: Average **2-6 weeks** to get a contractor started on major repairs (2023-2024 market)
  - **Permit processing for major repairs**: **1-4 weeks** in most jurisdictions
  - **Number of contractor bids typically obtained**: 3 (adds 2-4 weeks to decision process)
  - **Coordination for multi-trade repairs** (e.g., water damage involving demo, plumbing, drywall, paint):
    - Number of separate contractors: **3-5**
    - Total coordination overhead: **2-4 additional weeks** beyond pure work time
    - Scheduling gaps between trades: **3-7 days** average per handoff

### Source 7D: Warranty Claim Processes
- **Organization**: NAHB, RWC (Residential Warranty Company), 2-10 Home Buyers Warranty
- **Key Data Points**:
  - **Builder warranty standard coverage periods**:
    - Workmanship/materials: **1 year**
    - Systems (electrical, plumbing, HVAC): **2 years**
    - Structural: **10 years**
  - **Warranty claim frequency**:
    - Average new home generates **5-10 warranty claims in Year 1** (builder industry data)
    - **85-95%** of new homes have at least one warranty request in Year 1 (RWC data)
  - **Common warranty issues**:
    - Drywall cracking/nail pops: 65% of homes
    - HVAC performance issues: 30%
    - Plumbing leaks: 20%
    - Window/door operation: 25%
    - Grading/drainage: 15%
  - **Warranty claim processing timeline**:
    - Builder acknowledgment: **3-10 business days**
    - Inspection/assessment: **1-3 weeks** after acknowledgment
    - Repair scheduling: **2-6 weeks** after approval
    - Total timeline (report to resolution): **4-12 weeks typical**
  - **Third-party warranty claim process** (e.g., 2-10 HBW):
    - Filing to acknowledgment: **5-10 days**
    - Investigation/inspection: **2-4 weeks**
    - Decision: **1-3 weeks** after inspection
    - Repair coordination: **2-8 weeks**
    - Total: **6-16 weeks typical**, longer for structural claims
  - **Structural warranty claims**: Average resolution time **6-18 months** due to engineering assessments, repair plan development, and specialized contractor requirements
  - **Dispute rate**: **10-15%** of warranty claims are initially denied or disputed
  - **Escalation to third-party dispute resolution**: **3-5%** of all warranty claims

## 8. SUMMARY: FRICTION MAP DATA MATRIX

### Single-Family Home — Aggregate Friction Summary
(Based on ~8 month / 35-week average construction timeline)

| Friction Category | Time Impact | Cost Impact (% of total) |
|---|---|---|
| Rework | +3-4 weeks | 3-7% |
| Weather delays | +2-4 weeks | 2-4% (carrying costs) |
| Material delays | +2-4 weeks | 1-3% |
| Labor shortage delays | +3-6 weeks | 3-5% |
| Subcontractor coordination gaps | +3-6 weeks | 2-4% |
| Inspection wait & failures | +2-4 weeks | 1-2% |
| Change orders | +1-3 weeks | 5-10% |
| Communication/RFI processing | +1-2 weeks | 1-2% |
| **TOTAL FRICTION** | **+17-33 weeks** | **18-37%** |

> Note: Some friction categories overlap (e.g., labor delays and coordination gaps
> can run in parallel). The effective schedule friction is typically **40-75%** of the

> aligns with the observed increase from theoretical minimum (~16-20 weeks) to actual
